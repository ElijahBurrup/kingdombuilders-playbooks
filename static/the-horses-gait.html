Six Chapters on Pacing and Energy Management

Chapter One

"The Gallop Trap"

Blaze sprints across the field and pulls a tendon. Maren walks the same distance without breaking a sweat. Why operating at maximum intensity guarantees burnout. The Sprint Fallacy and the data on how chronic overwork destroys the very output it is trying to maximize.

Chapter Two

"The Four Gaits"

Walk (2-4 mph). Trot (8-12 mph). Canter (15-20 mph). Gallop (25-45 mph). Each gait has a specific biomechanical purpose and energy cost. The Four-Gear Framework for human performance: Admin, Focus, Deep Work, and Emergency.

Chapter Three

"The Transition"

The most dangerous moment for a horse is the gait transition. Stumbles happen between speeds, not within them. The Gear Change Protocol: how to shift between work modes cleanly and why context switching is the hidden tax destroying your productivity.

Chapter Four

"The Recovery Walk"

After every gallop, a horse must walk to cool down or it risks colic, which can be fatal. Active recovery is not optional — it is survival. The Cool-Down Protocol for high-intensity seasons: sabbath rhythms, deload weeks, and strategic rest.

Chapter Five

"The Endurance Horse"

Arabian horses win 100-mile endurance races not by being fastest but by managing their gaits. The winner averages 12 mph over 100 miles. The horse that sprints first finishes last. The Endurance Audit for long-term career and business sustainability.

Chapter Six

"The Rider's Hand"

A horse's gait is controlled by subtle signals from the rider. Learning to be your own rider: self-regulation, energy auditing, and the Weekly Gait Map for planning which speed each day and each task demands.
