The Raven's Trial

A story about eight steps, no instructions, and a bird that solves problems by watching them first . . . and why the AI agents reshaping your world work exactly the same way.

How AI Agents & Reasoning Work

KingdomBuilders AI

Eight steps. No instructions. No demonstration. She solved the puzzle by watching it once through the glass.

In a lab in Auckland, a raven named 007 faced an apparatus she had never seen. A piece of food hung from a string inside a box. To reach it, she needed to pull the string with her beak, pin it with her foot, pull again, pin again, and repeat until the food was close enough to grab. She had never been taught how to pull string. She had never been rewarded for pinning. She watched a researcher demonstrate the task once, through a window, and then she walked to the apparatus and executed the entire sequence on her first attempt.

This playbook is not about ravens. It is about the machines that reason the way she does.

If you finish this, you will understand how AI agents plan multiple steps before taking the first action, why an agent that thinks before it acts outperforms one that acts ten times faster, how tool selection determines whether an agent solves a problem or confidently fails at it, and why the difference between a chatbot and an agent is the willingness to be wrong mid-sequence and recover.

You will also learn something remarkable about corvids. They are not parroting solutions. They are reasoning through them. And the architecture that makes that possible is, at a structural level, the same architecture powering the AI agents that are beginning to book your flights, debug your code, and manage your calendar.

Chapter One
01
The Perch
Why the best agents spend more time thinking than doing.

"The plans of the diligent lead surely to abundance, but everyone who is hasty comes only to poverty." Proverbs 21:5

SCENE 1

Thirty Seconds of Nothing

The researchers placed a new apparatus on the table. A transparent tube filled with water. A piece of meat floating on the surface, too deep for a beak to reach. Three heavy stones and three light foam blocks arranged beside it.

Seven landed on the edge of the table. She did not touch anything. She turned her head left, then right, examining the tube from two angles. She looked at the stones. She looked at the foam. She looked at the water level. Thirty seconds passed. The researchers wrote "no interaction" in their notes.

Then she picked up a heavy stone, dropped it into the tube, watched the water rise, picked up the second heavy stone, dropped it, watched again, picked up the third, dropped it, and took the meat from the surface. She never touched the foam blocks. She never dropped a stone that was too light. Her first action was her correct action.

Installation video loading

Installation Thirty seconds of stillness. Then flawless execution. The pause is computation.

This is the most counterintuitive finding in corvid cognition research. The pause is not hesitation. It is computation.

When a raven faces a novel problem, she does not begin with trial and error. She begins with planning. Her eyes track the components. Her head tilts indicate binocular depth assessment. She is building an internal model of the problem: what connects to what, what moves what, what leads where.

AI agents work the same way. Before an agent takes any action, it generates what researchers call a chain of thought. This is the agent writing out its reasoning: "The user wants to book a flight to Austin. I need to know the dates. I should check their calendar first. Then search for flights. Then compare prices." The chain of thought is the perch. It is the thirty seconds of apparent inaction that makes the first action correct.

Visualization

The Perch in Action

Picture a raven on a branch above a puzzle. Her body is still. Her eyes are scanning. Inside her mind, she is running simulations: if I pull the string, the platform moves. If the platform moves, the stone rolls. If the stone rolls, it drops into the tube. She has not moved a muscle, but she has already solved the problem three times in her head.

Now picture an AI agent receiving a request: "Find me the cheapest direct flight to Austin next Friday and draft a confirmation email to my team." The agent pauses. It writes: "Step 1: Determine the date for next Friday. Step 2: Search flights to Austin on that date. Step 3: Filter for direct flights only. Step 4: Sort by price. Step 5: Draft email with flight details." It has not called a single tool yet. But it has already mapped the sequence.

See it: still body, moving eyes, a solved puzzle before the first touch.

Chapter Two
02
The Stone
Choosing the right tool changes everything. Choosing the wrong tool changes nothing.

"If the iron is blunt, and one does not sharpen the edge, he must use more strength, but wisdom helps one to succeed." Ecclesiastes 10:10

SCENE 2

The Weight Test

Researchers gave Seven a choice: heavy stones and light foam cubes. Both fit in the tube. Only one displaced enough water. She picked up a foam block, held it for a moment, set it down, and reached for the stone instead. She had never been taught which was heavier. She inferred it from handling.

Installation video loading

Installation Watch her evaluate, select, and commit. She never touches the foam.

This experiment, replicated across dozens of corvids, reveals something profound. Ravens do not just use tools. They evaluate tools against the requirements of the specific problem. A stone that worked in one tube might be too large for another. A stick that reached food in one box might be too short for a deeper one. The raven does not simply grab the nearest object. She selects.

AI tool use works identically. When an agent receives a question, it must decide which tool fits. A math question needs a calculator, not a web search. A question about today's weather needs a search engine, not a knowledge base. A coding task needs a code interpreter, not a text summary.

Search
Find current information from the web, databases, or documents
Code
Write and execute programs, scripts, data analysis
Calculator
Precise arithmetic, statistics, financial modeling
API Call
Interact with external services, send emails, manage calendars

The critical insight is that choosing the wrong tool does not just waste time. It produces confident wrong answers. If the raven drops foam into the tube, the water does not rise. She gets no food. If an agent uses web search to answer a math problem, it might find a similar problem with a different answer and present it confidently. The tool shapes the output. Wrong tool, wrong shape.

The raven does not try every stone. She evaluates, selects, and commits. The trial is not about effort. It is about judgment.

Chapter Three
03
The String
Each step only becomes visible after the previous one is complete.

"Your word is a lamp to my feet and a light to my path." Psalm 119:105

SCENE 3

Pull, Pin, Pull, Pin

The food hung from a long string dangling through a hole in the perch. Seven could see it. She could not reach it. She leaned down, gripped the string in her beak, pulled a length up, stepped on the loop to pin it, released her beak, reached down again, pulled another length, pinned it. Four repetitions. Each pull brought the food closer by six inches. She never let go without pinning first. She never pulled without releasing first. The sequence was flawless because each step created the conditions for the next.

Installation video loading

Installation Pull, pin, release, reach, pull again. Each step creates the next. This is chain of thought.

This is sequential reasoning. The raven does not see the solution as one action. She sees it as a chain where each link depends on the one before it. Pull creates slack. Pin preserves slack. Release frees the beak. Pull again extends the gain.

AI agents decompose tasks the same way. "Plan a weekend trip to Austin" is not one action. It is a sequence: check calendar for open dates, search flights for those dates, search hotels near the conference venue, calculate total budget, compare against spending limit, draft itinerary, send confirmation. No single step is difficult. The intelligence is in the decomposition.

1

Observe

The agent reads the request and identifies what it needs to accomplish.

2

Decompose

The complex goal is broken into ordered sub-tasks, each one achievable with a single tool call.

3

Execute

The first sub-task is performed. The result is observed.

4

Integrate

The result of step one becomes the input for step two. The chain continues.

5

Complete

The final sub-task's output satisfies the original goal.

The key principle: each step only becomes visible after the previous one completes. The raven cannot see how much string is left until she pulls the first length. The agent cannot search for hotels until it knows the travel dates. The sequence reveals itself one step at a time. This is why chain of thought reasoning is so powerful. By writing out each step explicitly, the agent makes the invisible chain visible.

Visualization

The String Pull

Imagine holding a rope attached to something you cannot see in a dark well. You pull six inches, pin the rope under your foot, and lean down to pull again. You do not know how deep the well is. You do not know how heavy the object is. You only know one thing: each pull brings it closer. The raven's string pull is a perfect model for how agents navigate uncertainty. You do not need to see the whole path. You need to see the next step and trust the sequence.

See it: pull, pin, release, reach, pull again. Each step creates the next.
You Already Know This

You do this every day. Making breakfast is sequential reasoning: you cannot pour coffee before the water boils, you cannot butter toast before it is toasted. You decompose without thinking about it. AI agents do the same thing, but with tasks like "analyze this quarterly report and email the highlights to the board."

Chapter Four
04
The Glass
She learned the solution by watching through a window. Not by being told.

"Apply your heart to instruction and your ears to words of knowledge." Proverbs 23:12

SCENE 4

The Window

In the most famous corvid experiment, a raven watched a researcher demonstrate a multi-step solution through a transparent barrier. She could see the actions but could not interact with the apparatus. When the barrier was removed, she walked directly to the apparatus and replicated the sequence. Not approximately. Exactly. She learned from observation, not from trial and error.

This is the training question. How did the raven learn without being taught? She observed patterns. She mapped actions to outcomes. She stored the relationship "this action leads to this result" without ever performing the action herself.

AI agents learn the same way. During training, the underlying language model processes billions of examples of human reasoning, problem solving, tool use, and error correction. It never performed these tasks itself. It watched through the glass. When the barrier is removed and a real task arrives, it draws on those observed patterns to generate a plan.

But here is the nuance the raven teaches us: observation is not imitation. Seven did not memorize a fixed sequence. She extracted the principle behind the sequence. When researchers changed the apparatus slightly (longer string, heavier food), she adapted her approach. She had learned the concept, not the recipe.

This is exactly what separates a good AI agent from a brittle one. A brittle agent memorizes: "When the user says X, call tool Y." A capable agent extracts the principle: "When the user needs current information, search for it. When the user needs computation, calculate it." The principle generalizes. The recipe breaks the moment the problem changes.

Chapter Five
05
The Miss
When the stone rolls away, she does not start over. She starts from where she is.

"For a righteous man falls seven times and rises again." Proverbs 24:16

SCENE 5

The Fallen Stone

In one trial, Seven dropped a stone toward the tube and missed. The stone bounced off the rim and rolled across the table. A less sophisticated animal might panic, restart, or abandon the task. Seven walked to where the stone had landed, picked it up, returned to the tube, and dropped it again. This time she positioned herself directly above the opening. She did not restart from the beginning. She corrected from the current state.

This is error recovery, and it is the capability that separates agents from scripts.

A script follows a fixed path. If step three fails, the script crashes. It has no concept of "current state." It only knows "expected state." When reality diverges from expectation, the script has nothing to say.

An agent, like Seven, operates differently. When a step fails, the agent does three things. First, it observes the new state: the stone is on the table, not in the tube. The water level has not changed. The food is still unreachable. Second, it diagnoses the failure: the stone missed because the drop angle was wrong. Third, it replans from the current position: walk to the stone, pick it up, reposition, drop again.

Did the action succeed?
YES
Proceed to the next step in the plan.
NO
Observe what actually happened.
Can I recover from here?
YES
Adjust the plan and retry from current state.
NO
Try an entirely different approach.

This is what happens when you ask an AI agent to search for information and the search returns no results. A bad agent will say "I couldn't find anything." A good agent will rewrite the search query with different keywords, try a different search tool, or ask you a clarifying question. It recovers from the current state. It does not pretend the failure did not happen, and it does not restart from scratch.

Something Just Happened

You have been recovering from failures your entire life without calling it "error recovery." When you take a wrong turn driving, you do not go home and start over. You recalculate from where you are. When a recipe goes wrong, you adjust, you do not throw away all the ingredients and start from the grocery store. The raven's recovery is your recovery. The AI agent's recovery is modeled on both.

The Real Cost of Not Recovering

Every time an agent restarts a task from the beginning instead of recovering from the failure point, it wastes all the work that came before the failure. If steps one through six succeeded and step seven failed, restarting discards six successful steps. Recovery preserves progress. Restart destroys it. This is why the raven walks to the fallen stone instead of going back to her perch. The progress is real. Only the last step needs correction.

Chapter Six
06
The Cache
She remembers what she hid. She also remembers who was watching when she hid it.

"The heart of the discerning acquires knowledge, for the ears of the wise seek it out." Proverbs 18:15

SCENE 6

The Watcher and the Thief

Seven buried a piece of food while another raven watched. Later, when the observer left, Seven returned to her cache, dug it up, and re-hid it in a new location. She did not re-hide caches that were buried in private. She remembered not just where she hid the food, but who was watching when she hid it.

Ravens maintain context in a way that goes beyond simple memory. They track what happened, who was present, and what that presence implies about future risk. This is not just storing facts. This is reasoning about the social and temporal dimensions of stored information.

AI agents face the same challenge through the context window. The context window is everything the agent can "see" at once: the conversation history, the results of previous tool calls, the user's original request, and its own reasoning so far. It is the agent's working memory.

But even 200,000 tokens has limits. On a complex task that spans dozens of tool calls, the context window fills up. Early steps get pushed out. The agent "forgets" what happened at the beginning. This is like a raven that has hidden food in fifty locations but can only remember the last twenty. The forgotten caches are lost.

The best agents manage their context the way Seven manages her caches. They summarize early results so the key information survives compression. They prioritize what to keep in active memory. And they know what they have forgotten. When Seven re-hides a cache, she is managing risk based on context. When an agent asks "Let me re-read the original request to make sure I haven't drifted," it is doing the same thing.

Visualization

The Context Cache

Imagine a desk with a fixed surface area. Every document you place on it is visible and usable. But the desk only fits fifty pages. When page fifty-one arrives, page one falls off the edge. You cannot make the desk bigger. You can only decide what stays on it. The agent's context window is that desk. The art of agency is deciding what to keep on the surface and what to let fall.

See it: a desk, fifty pages, and the constant decision of what stays visible.

Chapter Seven
07
The Trial
Eight steps. No instructions. One attempt. This is how agents reason.

"I have fought the good fight, I have finished the race, I have kept the faith." 2 Timothy 4:7

SCENE 7

The Eight Step Puzzle

The final apparatus combined everything. To reach the food, Seven needed to: (1) pull a short stick from a cage, (2) use that stick to reach a longer stick in a second cage, (3) use the longer stick to push a stone off a ledge, (4) let the stone drop into a tube, (5) watch the water rise, (6) pull the string now within reach, (7) pin the string, (8) take the food. Eight steps. Three tools. Two physics principles. One raven.

She paused on her perch. She looked at each component. Then she began.

She did not make a single error.

This is the complete agent loop. Everything from the previous six chapters converges here. The perch (planning). The stone (tool selection). The string (sequential reasoning). The glass (learned patterns). The miss (error recovery, though she did not need it). The cache (maintaining context across eight steps).

Modern AI agents run this exact loop. A user asks: "Analyze our company's Q3 sales data, identify the three weakest product lines, research what competitors are doing in those categories, and draft a strategy memo for the executive team." The agent does not panic at the complexity. It perches. It plans. It selects tools. It executes step by step. It recovers from errors. It maintains context. And it produces a result that no single tool call could have generated.

Why This Matters for You

AI agents are not a future technology. They are here. They are booking travel, writing code, analyzing data, managing email, and coordinating workflows. The question is not whether they will change how you work. The question is whether you understand them well enough to use them wisely and build with them intelligently.

The raven's trial is not about a bird solving a puzzle. It is about a pattern of intelligence that works whether the brain is biological or digital: observe, plan, select, execute, recover, remember, complete.
