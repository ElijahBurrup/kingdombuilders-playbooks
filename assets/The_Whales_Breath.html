Aria took a breath at the surface. The Pacific stretched in every direction, gray green, immense, alive with currents she could feel in her jaw but could not see. She was eighteen months old, large enough now to dive alone.

The stopped process is one of Gendlin's most powerful concepts, and it maps directly onto one of diving's most dangerous phenomena: the bends.

Here is the physiology. Under pressure at depth, nitrogen from the air you breathe dissolves into your blood and tissues, the way carbon dioxide dissolves into water under pressure to make sparkling water. At depth, you do not feel this. The pressure keeps the nitrogen in solution. But if you ascend too fast, if you rush toward the surface, the pressure drops faster than the body can equalize. The dissolved nitrogen comes out of solution and forms bubbles in your blood. Gas bubbles in your bloodstream. They block blood vessels. They cause excruciating joint pain, neurological damage, paralysis, death.

The cure for the bends is not to push harder toward the surface. The cure is to go back down. To descend. To repressurize the body and then ascend slowly, with decompression stops at specific depths, allowing the nitrogen to leave the blood gradually, safely, at the body's own rate.

This is Gendlin's stopped process, rendered in physiology. When the body's forward movement is blocked, the worst thing you can do is force it. Thrashing. Grinding. More hours. More effort. The same direction, with more force. This is ascending too fast. It produces the bends: pain, damage, breakdown.
