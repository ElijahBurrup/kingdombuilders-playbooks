Chapter One
01

Taste

Curry knows mid-release whether the shot is good. Most people cannot tell after watching the ball land. The first fundamental is the eye.

If you watch Curry shoot a thousand times, the strangest thing is what happens between release and result. The ball has left his hand. There are still two full seconds before it arrives at the rim. In that two second window, Curry is no longer watching the ball. He is jogging back on defense. He is calling out a coverage. Sometimes he is celebrating before the ball lands.

This is not arrogance. This is taste. The ball left his fingertips at exactly the right release height, exactly the right backspin, exactly the right arc. His body knew before the result arrived. Most amateurs watch the ball intently because they cannot tell. The professional cannot tell whether the shot will go in. The master cannot tell whether the shot is good.

The Master

The Five Hundred Shots Curry Rates, Not Just Takes

Curry's father Dell, an NBA shooter himself, drilled one thing harder than the shot: rate every shot. After every release, Stephen had to say one of three words. Pure. Off. Why. If "pure" he banked the rep. If "off" he had to name what was off before the next one. The Currys were not training the shot. They were training the judgment of the shot.

By the time Stephen reached the NBA, the judgment had collapsed into the body. He no longer needed to say the word. The shot left his hand and he was the rating. This is what taste means in any domain. The aesthetic judgment fuses with the action so completely that the two become one event.

For AI Work

The Taste Fundamental

The hardest skill in the AI era is not generating output. Generation is free. The hardest skill is distinguishing genuinely good AI output from confident slop. Most users cannot. They accept the first thing that arrives because it looks plausible. They ship the average. The market gets flooded with mediocre work that looks fine because nobody is rating shots.

The master rates every shot. Three pieces of AI output came back from the same prompt. One of them is better than the other two. Why? Naming the why is the rep. The why becomes vocabulary. The vocabulary becomes taste. Taste collapses into judgment that fires before the result is even fully read.

This is the fundamental that protects you against the generic. Everyone has the same models. Only some people can tell which output to ship.

The Daily Drill
12 min
The Three Outputs Rating

Pick one real task you actually need done today. Email, summary, outline, code, recipe. The task itself does not matter.

  1. Generate the same output three times. Same prompt, three runs. If your tool has model selection, run the same prompt against three different models.
  2. Read all three carefully. Rank them best to worst.
  3. Write one sentence about why the best one beat the others. The sentence must use a specific concrete word. Not "better tone." Say "more concrete," "less hedging," "the example was real," "the structure went general-to-specific instead of specific-to-general." Vague reasons do not build taste. Specific reasons do.
  4. Write one sentence about why the worst one is the worst. Same rule. Specific concrete word.
  5. Keep a running list. After thirty days you will have sixty specific named flaws and virtues. That list is your taste lexicon. After ninety days the lexicon will start firing automatically while you read.

Curry's father did not tell Stephen what good shots looked like. He made Stephen rate them and explain why. The rating is the rep. Do not skip the why.

Stop and Notice

How many AI outputs have you shipped without rating?

Of the last ten things you accepted from an AI, how many did you explicitly rank against alternatives before sending? The honest answer is the size of your taste deficit.

Chapter Two
02

Naming

Jiro can identify a flaw from one grain. He spent seventy years naming imperfections. The second fundamental is the precision of the word.

In a basement subway sushi bar in Tokyo, Jiro Ono spent seventy years making nigiri. By the time he was old enough that his hands trembled at rest, the moment he picked up rice the tremor stopped. The rice required his hands to be steady, and his hands obeyed.

The astonishing thing about Jiro is not the sushi. It is what he can name. He can name the difference between rice cooked at 96 degrees and rice cooked at 97. He can name the rate at which a piece of tuna releases oil at different temperatures. He can name twelve specific imperfections in a single grain. His apprentices spend ten years on rice alone before they are allowed to touch fish. The ten years is not for the rice. It is for the names.

The Master

The Ten Year Rice Apprenticeship

Jiro's son Yoshikazu trained under him for over a decade. For most of those years his only job was rice. Wash, measure, cook, season, shape, taste. Wash, measure, cook, season, shape, taste. After eight years, Jiro pulled him aside and said: "The rice is ready."

What had Yoshikazu actually learned? Not how to make rice. He had learned to name what was wrong with rice. Too soft. Too dry. Vinegar penetration uneven. Grain temperature dropping faster on the surface. Compression artifacts from his thumb. He had built a vocabulary of imperfection that allowed him to see what no untrained eye could see. The vocabulary was the skill. The skill was the vocabulary.

For AI Work

The Naming Fundamental

The difference between a vague prompt and a surgical prompt is not effort. It is vocabulary. The amateur writes "give me an email to my client about the project delay." The master writes "give me a 90 word email to a client I have worked with for 4 years explaining a 2 week delay caused by a vendor issue, accepting full responsibility, naming a specific recovery date, written in our usual warm-but-direct register, no apologies for the apology."

The first prompt produces generic email. The second produces a specific email. The model is the same. The naming is the leverage.

Most people have a sloppy vocabulary because they have never been forced to be precise. Naming is the rep that turns fuzzy thinking into surgical thinking. The model only knows what you say. You only get good output when you can say what you actually want.

The Daily Drill
10 min
The 25 / 15 / 5 Funnel

Pick one fuzzy thing you actually want today. A piece of work, an outcome, a feeling, a relationship adjustment, a deliverable. Anything currently vague in your head.

  1. Write what you want in exactly 25 words. No more. This is the first compression. You will discover what you do not actually know.
  2. Now compress to exactly 15 words. You will be forced to delete the noise and keep the signal. This is uncomfortable. The discomfort is the rep.
  3. Compress one more time. Exactly 5 words. No verbs allowed unless the verb is the point. The 5 word version is the name of what you want.
  4. Prompt the AI using the 5 word name as the title and the 25 word version as the spec. The 15 word version is your check: if the AI output does not satisfy that sentence, you have not gotten what you asked for.
  5. Note the friction. Where did the compression hurt most? That is the place your thinking was actually fuzzy.

The 5 word version is what Jiro would call the dish. The 25 word version is the recipe. If you cannot name it in five words, you do not know what you want. The AI cannot help you find out.

Chapter Three
03

Decomposition

Bannister did not run a four minute mile. He ran four perfect quarter miles. The third fundamental is breaking the impossible into doable.

Before May 6, 1954, doctors said the four minute mile was beyond human physiology. Hearts would burst. Lungs would collapse. The barrier was treated as a biological fact, not a record. Then a 25 year old medical student named Roger Bannister ran it in 3:59.4. Within two months, three other men also broke four minutes. Within ten years, schoolboys were doing it. What changed?

The biology did not change. The naming did, and the decomposition did. Bannister did not train to run a four minute mile. He trained to run four perfect quarter miles, each in 59 seconds. He stopped thinking about a mile entirely. He thought only about the next quarter, then the next, then the next. The lap was the unit. The mile was the side effect.

The Master

The Mile Was An Accident Of Four Laps

Bannister was a medical student with limited training time, no coach for years, and a self designed program. While the established runners worked on stamina at the mile distance, Bannister did relentless interval work at the quarter mile. Ten times 400 meters at 60 seconds, with two minute recoveries. Then nine. Then eight. He drilled the unit, not the goal.

On the day of the race, his pacers brought him through 0.75 miles in 3:00.7. The final quarter was the only one he ran alone, and he ran it in 58.9. Add the four splits: 57.5, 60.7, 62.3, 58.9. The mile was 3:59.4. He never ran a mile in training under four minutes. He only ran the splits. The mile was what happened when the splits aligned.

For AI Work

The Decomposition Fundamental

Most prompts fail because the problem is too big. "Build me a marketing strategy for my new product launch" is a mile. The AI tries. The output is generic because no model can do the whole mile in one stride. The amateur reads the generic output and concludes the AI is not good enough. The amateur is wrong. The amateur never decomposed.

The master breaks the mile into laps. "List 8 specific customer pain points the product solves, with one concrete example each." Lap one. "For each pain point, write 3 short positioning statements." Lap two. "Pick the strongest statement and write 5 ad variations against it." Lap three. "For each ad, write the 3 hooks most likely to land for our audience profile." Lap four. The mile assembles itself from the laps.

The skill is finding the smallest atomic step the AI can actually nail. Most users never zoom in that far. They aim at the goal. The master aims at the unit.

The Daily Drill
15 min
The Five Step Pipeline

Take any real task that is currently bigger than one prompt. A project. An analysis. A piece of writing. A decision you have been avoiding because it feels too big to start.

  1. Write the goal in one sentence. Be clear about what done looks like.
  2. Now write the smallest possible first step the AI could fully complete in one turn. Not the first big chunk. The first atomic step. The decomposition is good when the step is almost embarrassingly small.
  3. Write step two. Step two takes step one's output as input. Use the actual phrase "using the output above" in your prompt so you are forced to chain them.
  4. Continue to step five. By the time you reach step five, the original goal should be visibly closer or done.
  5. Run the pipeline. Note which step was the bottleneck. The bottleneck is the lap you need to drill again until it runs in under one minute.

Bannister did not run the mile. He ran the laps. You do not prompt the project. You prompt the lap.

Stop and Decompose

Name one project that has been stuck because it felt too big.

What is the smallest first step the AI could fully complete in one turn? If you cannot answer that in 30 seconds, the project is not stuck because of the project. It is stuck because of the decomposition.

Chapter Four
04

Iteration

The Incredibles went through 27 versions of every scene. The amateur ships turn one. The master is on turn ten.

Brad Bird directed The Incredibles. Late in production, he gave a talk that has been quoted in design schools ever since. He said something close to this: the difference between Pixar and other studios is one number. Other studios go to version three of a scene. Pixar goes to version twenty. The films that look effortless took twenty drafts. There was a version one of The Incredibles. It was, in Bird's own words, "terrible." The audience does not see version one. The audience sees version twenty seven.

Pixar calls it plussing. Every meeting, every reviewer adds something to make the current version slightly better. The director's job is to keep the iteration going long after the team thinks they are done. The team's job is to not lose heart through draft eleven.

The Master

The Toy Story 2 Rebuild

In 1998, Pixar was in production on Toy Story 2 as a direct-to-video sequel. After eight months, they screened the cut. It was bad. Pixar leadership made a stunning call. They threw out the entire film and rebuilt it from scratch in nine months. The completely rebuilt Toy Story 2 grossed nearly half a billion dollars and is widely considered the rarest of things: a sequel that surpasses the original.

What Pixar understood that other studios did not is that first attempts are not real attempts. They are scaffolding. The point of a first attempt is to reveal the shape of the problem. The real work begins on attempt two. The breakthrough often arrives somewhere around attempt twelve. Studios that ship first attempts ship scaffolding.

For AI Work

The Iteration Fundamental

Studies of how people use AI consistently find the same pattern. The median user has a conversation length of one to two turns. They prompt. They receive. They accept or they leave. The bottom half of users average less than two turns per task.

The master is doing something completely different. The master treats turn one as scaffolding. Turn one's job is not to produce the output. Turn one's job is to reveal what is wrong with the prompt. The output is read for what it gets wrong. Turn two corrects. Turn three refines. By turn ten, the master has steered the model into producing something the model could not have produced in any single turn.

Most of the magic AI work being produced right now is happening at turn 8 or beyond. Most users will never see that work because they quit at turn 2.

The Daily Drill
15 min
The Ten Turn Refinement

Take a real output you got in one or two turns this week and accepted. Open a fresh conversation. Bring the original task back.

  1. Turn one. The original prompt. Generate output.
  2. Turn two. Identify the single weakest part of the output. Ask for that part to be rewritten.
  3. Turn three. Identify the next weakest part. Same move.
  4. Turns four through seven. One specific improvement per turn. Tone. Structure. Specificity. Examples. Length. Each turn fixes one thing.
  5. Turn eight. Ask the AI to critique its own current output as if it were grading someone else's work. The critique is now your spec.
  6. Turn nine. Have it incorporate the critique.
  7. Turn ten. Compare to the original turn one output. Note the gap. The gap is your iteration deficit.

Pixar's first draft of The Incredibles was, by their own admission, terrible. Your first turn is too. The product is not turn one. The product is turn ten.

Chapter Five
05

Synthesis

Hamilton was not new history, not new hip-hop, not new theater. Miranda spent six years combining three known things into one unknown thing.

Lin-Manuel Miranda was on vacation in 2008 and picked up a book by Ron Chernow about Alexander Hamilton. By page 30 he was scribbling. By page 100 he had already written the first song. The book's premise was that Hamilton was a self-invented immigrant who hustled his way to power through sheer verbal velocity. Miranda's brain did the strange thing. This man is a hip hop artist.

It took six more years to finish the musical. Hamilton premiered in 2015 and became one of the most acclaimed works in Broadway history. The shocking thing about it is that not a single component was new. Founding Father biographies existed. Hip hop existed. Broadway musicals existed. R&B existed. Miranda did not invent any of the parts. He spent six years synthesizing them into one thing that did not exist before.

The Master

Six Years In The Synthesis Room

Miranda wrote and discarded entire songs. He sent demos to his friend Tommy Kail every few months and Kail would tell him what was not working. He read every Hamilton biography. He listened to hours of historical recordings and modern rap. The work was not the writing. The work was the matching. Which hip hop verse structure fit the cabinet battle? Which R&B ballad form fit the death of Hamilton's son? Which Broadway tradition could carry the duel?

What Miranda was doing was synthesis as repeated experiment. He kept generating combinations and rating them. Most failed. A few clicked. The clicks were the show. The synthesis was the genius. None of the parts were.

For AI Work

The Synthesis Fundamental

Most people use AI as a single-shot oracle. One prompt, one output, take it or leave it. The master uses AI as a synthesizer's keyboard. Run the same prompt against three different framings. Run it against two different models. Take an output and feed it back to a different model as the input. Watch what happens when you ask the same question to a model trained on technical writing versus a model trained on conversational writing.

The original work is not what comes out of any single prompt. The original work is the synthesis across multiple prompts. You generate the parts. You combine the parts. The combination is what nobody else could have written, because nobody else ran your specific synthesis.

This is the place where AI work stops feeling derivative and starts feeling like yours. Miranda's parts were not original. The synthesis was the entire achievement.

The Daily Drill
15 min
The Three Source Combine

Pick one task today where you want output that feels original, not generic. Writing, ideas, a plan, an explanation, an angle on a problem.

  1. Generate the same thing three different ways. Different model if available. Different framing in each prompt. (Example: prompt A asks for it analytical. Prompt B asks for it as a story. Prompt C asks for it as a list of contradictions.)
  2. Read all three. Highlight the single strongest line, idea, or paragraph in each. Just one per source.
  3. Write a fresh prompt that says: "Combine these three elements into one coherent piece" and paste your three highlights.
  4. Read the synthesis. Edit by hand to make it sound like you.
  5. Note: the final piece is not what any single prompt produced. It is the synthesis that only you could have built because only you picked those three highlights.

Hamilton is three known things assembled in a previously unknown way. The synthesis is the originality. The parts were always going to be there. The combination was waiting for the combiner.

Stop and Combine

What are the three known fields you should be synthesizing across?

Most original work in the AI age will come from people who can hold two or three domains at once and combine them through the model. Name three you already know well. The synthesis lives where they overlap.

Chapter Six
06

Verification

When the Hubble launched, every image was blurry. The team found a flaw one fiftieth the width of a human hair and fixed it in orbit. The sixth fundamental is comparing output to reality before you ship.

NASA launched the Hubble Space Telescope in April 1990. It cost 1.5 billion dollars. It was meant to be the most precise eye ever pointed at the universe. When the first images came back, they were blurry. Every single one. The most expensive scientific instrument in history was producing pictures worse than a backyard telescope.

The team that diagnosed the problem, including an astronomer named Marcia Rieke, did not panic. They verified every pixel against the expected models of starlight. By comparing the actual output to what the optics should have produced, they isolated the flaw. The Hubble's primary mirror had been ground to the wrong shape by one fiftieth the width of a human hair. That microscopic error blurred every image. They designed a corrective lens, sent a shuttle mission up to install it in 1993, and the Hubble began producing the iconic images that defined modern astronomy.

The Master

Comparing Output To Reality

The Hubble team did not assume the telescope was right. They assumed there was an unknown reality and the output was an approximation of it. Their job was to compare. They had calibration stars whose light output was known. They had physics models that predicted what each pixel should record. When the actual recorded value diverged from the model, they knew something was wrong upstream of the sensor.

This is the most undertrained skill in the AI era. The model produces something. It looks plausible. Plausible is not the same as true. The master never confuses the two. The amateur ships what is plausible. The master compares plausible to verified and only ships what survives.

For AI Work

The Verification Fundamental

AI hallucinates. It will produce confident citations of books that do not exist, statistics that were never measured, quotes attributed to people who never said them. The output looks right because the model is fluent. Fluency is the enemy of verification. Fluent wrong is more dangerous than obvious wrong.

The master picks specific anchor points in every output and verifies them. One factual claim. One logical step. One specific number. The verification is not exhaustive. It is targeted. You are not auditing the whole piece. You are sampling for hallucination the way the Hubble team sampled pixels against calibration stars.

If the sample passes, you have evidence the whole is probably trustworthy. If the sample fails, you have caught the flaw before you shipped a blurry telescope. Most AI mistakes that have damaged careers in the last two years would have been caught by one minute of verification.

The Daily Drill
10 min
The Three Anchor Check

Before you ship any AI output that has any factual content, pick three anchors.

  1. Anchor one. A specific factual claim. A date. A name. A quoted phrase. Open a primary source and verify it. Wikipedia is a starting point, not an ending point. Click through to the cited source.
  2. Anchor two. A specific number or statistic. Where did the model get this? Search for the number. If you cannot find an authoritative source within two minutes, the number is probably wrong or invented.
  3. Anchor three. A logical leap. Find one place in the output where the model concluded something. Ask: would a careful reader agree that step follows from the prior step? If you have to argue for it, the leap is suspicious.
  4. If any of the three anchors fails, treat the entire output as suspect. Verify more, or rewrite the section.
  5. Keep a log. After thirty days you will have ninety verifications. You will know your model's hallucination patterns: which kinds of claims it gets right and which kinds it routinely invents. That pattern recognition is verification mastery.

The Hubble team did not assume their billion dollar telescope was right. You do not have a billion dollar telescope. The verification is the difference between the master and the embarrassment.
