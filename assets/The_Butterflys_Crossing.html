The Last Leaf

Monarch was eating her last leaf. She did not know it was the last one, not the way you know a fact, but the way you know a feeling. Something had been building inside her for days, a pressure that had nothing to do with hunger, a sense that the milkweed she had been eating since the hour she hatched was no longer the point.

Every cell in her body was whispering a word she did not have a name for. The word was forward.

She was afraid. Not of predators. Not of the wind. She was afraid of the thing inside her that implied dissolution. She could feel it the way you feel a wave approaching before it arrives, the pull of the water receding from your feet, the silence before the sound. Something was about to happen that would unmake everything she recognized as herself.

"I do not want to go," she said to the leaf. The leaf did not answer.

That was when Drift landed on the branch. A Monarch butterfly, wings the color of stained glass, orange and black and white arranged in patterns that seemed to contain messages Monarch could not read. Drift folded her wings slowly, like someone closing a book they had memorized.

"You will not become a better caterpillar. You will carry forward into something that changes what 'you' means."

This is Gendlin's concept of carrying forward, and it is one of the most important ideas in the philosophy of process. Carrying forward is not continuation in a straight line. It is not repetition. It is not improvement, optimization, or iteration. It is a process moving into its next step in a way that transforms everything that came before, including the meaning of the starting point itself.

Consider what actually happens to the caterpillar. It does not grow wings while keeping its legs. It does not gradually sprout antennae while retaining its mandibles. It dissolves. Its entire body liquefies into a soup of undifferentiated cells, and from that soup, an entirely new architecture assembles itself. The butterfly is not a modified caterpillar. It is what the caterpillar was always implying, the next step that the caterpillar's body was pointing toward from the moment the egg hatched.

But here is the part that breaks most people's understanding: the butterfly does not replace the caterpillar. The butterfly carries the caterpillar forward. Research published in PLOS ONE demonstrated that moths trained to avoid certain odors as caterpillars retained those aversions after metamorphosis. The learning survived the complete destruction and reconstruction of the nervous system. The carrying forward was so fundamental that it persisted through total dissolution.

Inside the Dissolution

Inside the chrysalis, Monarch dissolved. There is no gentler way to say it. Her body, every organ, every muscle, every nerve fiber, released its structure and became liquid. Not metaphorically. Actually, physically, biologically liquid. What remained was a suspension of cells called imaginal cells, floating in the enzymatic soup of what used to be a living creature.

Imaginal cells. The name itself contains the insight. These cells carry the image of the butterfly, not as a blueprint stored somewhere, but as a pattern of self-organization that was always latent in the caterpillar's biology. They were present from the beginning, dormant, waiting. They did not need instructions from the outside. They needed the old structure to dissolve so they could organize without interference.

If you could look inside the chrysalis at this moment, and scientists have, using micro-CT scanning, you would see something that looks like chaos. A formless mass with no recognizable anatomy. No head. No thorax. No legs. Nothing that tells you what this thing used to be or what it is about to become.

And it is the most creative moment in the entire life cycle.

This is the moment Gendlin describes as the space between, the gap between one form of experiencing and the next. The old framework has dissolved. The new framework has not yet crystallized. You are formless. You are undefined. You have released the identity that organized your life and the new identity has not yet assembled itself.

Most people flee this space. They cannot tolerate the formlessness, the ambiguity, the not-knowing. They grab for the old form, they try to reassemble the caterpillar from its dissolved components. Or they grab for any new form, any structure at all, rather than endure the suspension. The person leaving a career who immediately jumps into another career without pausing. The person ending a relationship who immediately starts another. The company abandoning a strategy and latching onto the first alternative rather than sitting in the uncertainty.

But Gendlin insists that this space is not empty. The dissolution is not destruction. It is full of implying. The imaginal cells know what they are becoming even though there is no visible structure yet. The pattern is organizing itself beneath the threshold of recognizable form. The forward step is being assembled in the darkness.

The Biology of the Between

What actually happens during dissolution

Enzymatic breakdown. The caterpillar's body releases enzymes that digest its own tissues. Muscles, nerves, organs, everything except the imaginal discs, is broken down into cellular soup. This is not damage. This is intentional self-deconstruction.

Immune resistance. The caterpillar's immune system attacks the imaginal cells as foreign invaders. The old form literally fights the emerging new form. But the imaginal cells persist, connect with each other, and begin forming clusters.

Memory transfer. Through mechanisms science still does not fully understand, learned behaviors survive the complete dissolution of the nervous system. The carrying forward is so deep that it persists through total destruction of the hardware that encoded it.

Pattern emergence. From the undifferentiated soup, structure begins to crystallize. Wings first. Then thorax. Then compound eyes. The new architecture assembles according to an entirely different organizational principle than the caterpillar's.

"I press on toward the goal to win the prize for which God has called me heavenward in Christ Jesus.". Philippians 3:14

The First Flight

Monarch emerged from the chrysalis on a Tuesday morning when the milkweed was in bloom. She hung from the empty shell of her former self, wings crumpled and wet, pumping hemolymph into veins that had never existed before. Two hours of stillness. Two hours of becoming.

And then she flew.

But she did not fly in a straight line. No butterfly does. She zigzagged. Forward twenty feet, then a sharp turn back toward the milkweed where she had spent her caterpillar life. She hovered over the plant, investigating it with compound eyes that processed light in ways her simple caterpillar ocelli never could. The milkweed looked different. Familiar in its chemistry. Alien in its appearance through these new eyes.

She flew forward again. Toward wildflowers she had never visited as a caterpillar. Then back. Then forward to a different patch. Then back to the milkweed again. Each backward reference changed what she found there. Each forward movement was informed by the backward glance.

The path was not a line. It was a zigzag. And the zigzag was creating something that neither the forward nor the backward contained alone.

Gendlin describes this as the zig-zag: the characteristic movement of any genuine carrying forward. The process moves into something new (the zig), then refers back to what was implied before the step was taken (the zag), and discovers that the backward reference has changed. What you find when you look back is not what was there before you moved forward. The forward step transforms the backward reference. And the transformed backward reference creates a new forward direction.

This is not revision. It is not correcting your memory. It is something far stranger and far more important. When you carry an experience forward, by speaking it, by acting on it, by entering a new context, the experience itself changes. Not your interpretation of it. The experience. Gendlin is making a radical claim: the past is not fixed. Not because the events change, but because the meaning of those events is constituted by the process that carries them forward. And when the process moves, the meaning moves with it.

Consider this: you have an unclear feeling, a bodily sense that something in your life is not right. You sit with it. You try to speak it. You say: "I think I am angry at my partner." The moment you say it, the feeling shifts. It was not anger, exactly. It was something more complicated. The act of articulating it (the zig) changes what you find when you refer back to the feeling (the zag). Now the feeling is clearer but also different. You try again: "It is not anger. It is grief." The new articulation shifts the feeling again. Each forward step transforms the backward reference. Each backward reference creates a new forward step.

The Landing

On the third day after emergence, Monarch found a patch of black-eyed Susans at the edge of a meadow. She had been feeding, her new proboscis uncoiling into flowers to drink nectar, a sensation so unlike chewing leaves that it felt like using a sense she had never possessed before.

"Watch what happens when you land," Drift said, settling onto a purple coneflower beside her.

Monarch landed on a black-eyed Susan. Her legs gripped the petals. Her proboscis uncurled into the center. She drank. While she drank, her legs and abdomen picked up grains of pollen, sticky, golden particles that clung to the fine hairs on her body.

"Now fly to the next one."

She lifted off and landed on a coneflower ten feet away. As she drank, the pollen from the first flower brushed against the stigma. Fertilization was initiated. Seeds would form. New plants would grow next season in soil where no black-eyed Susans had grown before.

Drift said quietly: "What happened between you and the flower is not a combination of butterfly-hunger plus flower-reproduction. It is something entirely new. Something neither of you contained. Something that only exists because two independent processes crossed."

This is Gendlin's concept of crossing, and it may be the most radical idea in A Process Model. Crossing occurs when two independently ongoing processes interact, and the interaction produces something that neither process contained, implied, or could have generated alone. The butterfly's hunger and the flower's reproduction are completely independent processes. They evolved separately. They serve different purposes. But when they cross, when the butterfly lands on the flower, something new emerges: the ecosystem itself. Pollination. Biodiversity. The co-evolution of flower color and insect vision. None of these existed in the butterfly's hunger or the flower's reproductive strategy. They emerged from the crossing.

Crossing is not combination. A combination takes two existing things and puts them together: chocolate plus vanilla is a combination. The result contains exactly what the inputs contained, just mixed together. Crossing is fundamentally different. The result of a crossing contains something that was in neither input. The ecosystem is not butterfly-hunger plus flower-reproduction. It is a genuinely new thing that emerged from the crossing but exists independently of either process.

Crossing is not compromise, either. A compromise takes two competing positions and finds the middle ground. The result is less than either input. Crossing does not average. It generates. What emerges from crossing is more than the sum, not less. It is qualitatively new, not quantitatively split.

Combination

"Flour + water = paste."

You can predict the result from the ingredients. Nothing surprising. It is additive. 1 + 1 = 2. Most "collaborations" are this. Two people contributing their parts. The sum is the sum.

Crossing

"Biology + computing = bioinformatics."

An entirely new field with its own questions, methods, and discoveries that nobody could have derived from either parent. 1 + 1 = something that didn't exist before.

On the fifth day, Monarch found a still pool at the edge of the meadow. She had been avoiding water, an instinct carried forward from caterpillar days, when rain was dangerous and puddles were drowning hazards. But now she was a creature of the air, and water was a mirror.
